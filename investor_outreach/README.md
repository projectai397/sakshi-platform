## Tips for Success

### Tip 1: Start Immediately

Don't wait for "perfect" materials. Start outreach as soon as you have a solid deck and financial model. You can refine materials based on investor feedback during the process.

### Tip 2: Maintain Momentum

Fundraising requires sustained effort. Block your calendar for investor activities and don't let other responsibilities distract you. Momentum is criticalâ€”investors want to see progress.

### Tip 3: Create Competitive Dynamics

Engage multiple investors simultaneously. Share that you have other interested parties (without naming names). This creates urgency and prevents low-ball offers.

### Tip 4: Focus on Strategic Fit

Choose investors based on strategic value, not just terms. An investor who brings domain expertise, portfolio company introductions, and network access is worth more than a purely financial investor offering a slightly higher valuation.

### Tip 5: Be Authentic

Don't try to be someone you're not. Investors invest in people, not just ideas. Show your passion, your expertise, and your authentic self. Vulnerability builds trust.

### Tip 6: Learn from Rejection

Not every investor will say yes. That's okay. Ask for feedback, learn from it, and improve your pitch. Rejection is part of the process.

### Tip 7: Celebrate Progress

Fundraising is hard and emotionally draining. Celebrate small wins along the wayâ€”first meeting scheduled, first term sheet received, round closed. Acknowledge your progress.

### Tip 8: Take Care of Yourself

Fundraising is a marathon, not a sprint. Get adequate sleep, exercise, and downtime. Burnout helps no one. You need to be at your best for investor meetings.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Talking Too Much

Investors want dialogue, not monologue. Leave time for questions and listen carefully to their concerns. The best pitches are conversations, not presentations.

### Mistake 2: Being Defensive

When investors raise concerns, don't get defensive. Acknowledge valid points, provide data-driven responses, and show you've thought deeply about risks.

### Mistake 3: Lack of Specificity

Avoid vague language like "we're targeting the wellness market" or "we'll acquire customers through marketing." Use specific numbers, names, and examples.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Red Flags

If an investor is consistently unresponsive, asks surface-level questions, or shows low engagement, they're probably not interested. Move on quickly.

### Mistake 5: Not Asking for the Investment

Always end with a clear ask: "We're raising â‚¹10 crores for 20% equity. Based on our conversation, would [INVESTOR_NAME] like to participate?" Don't be afraid of the answer.

### Mistake 6: Overpromising

Don't make unrealistic promises about growth, returns, or timelines. Be conservative in projections and honest about risks. Underpromise and overdeliver.

### Mistake 7: Poor Energy Management

Don't schedule too many meetings in one day. You need to bring energy and enthusiasm to every meeting. Schedule breaks and take care of yourself.
